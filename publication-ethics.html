Publication Ethics

1. Authorship 2. Conflict of interest 3. Complaints & appeals 4. Research misconduct 5. Plagiarism 6. Informed consent 7. Citation manipulation 8. Withdrawal & retraction
1
Authorship

Authorship should be reserved for those who made significant contributions to the research — encompassing its conception, design, execution, or interpretation. Others who provided substantial support, such as language editing, should be acknowledged separately.

The corresponding author is responsible for ensuring accurate and appropriate co-authorship, confirming all listed contributors deserve inclusion, no deserving person is omitted, and everyone has reviewed and approved the final manuscript.

Careful consideration of authorship order and inclusion is expected before submission. Any changes involving addition, deletion, or rearrangement of authors will only be considered by the editor in exceptional circumstances. All authors collectively share responsibility for the work.

2
Conflict of interest

All potential conflicts of interest must be disclosed by the editor before taking office. Once appointed, the editor must recuse themselves from decision-making regarding papers in which they, family members, or close colleagues have authorship or vested interest.

Reviewers with potential conflicts due to professional, collaborative, or personal connections with the authors should consult the editor before agreeing to participate. Any suggestion to cite their own work must be based solely on genuine scientific merit.

Authors should disclose any financial or personal ties that could be perceived as inappropriately biasing their work, including employment, consulting roles, stock ownership, honoraria, patent applications, and grants.

3
Complaints and appeals

In the event of a rejected article where compelling grounds exist to contest the decision, such as new evidence or a potential misinterpretation by a reviewer, you have the right to appeal the editorial judgment.

  1. 1
    Submit a written appeal to the journal editor, clearly stating grounds and supporting evidence.
  2. 2
    The editorial team will carefully consider your appeal with thorough attention.
  3. 3
    You'll receive acknowledgment within 10 days. Final decision is aimed within 60 days.
  4. 4
    The final decision is communicated in writing and is final — the same article may not be appealed more than once.
4
Allegations of research misconduct

CTUJoISD takes all allegations of research misconduct seriously and is committed to a fair, unbiased, and timely response. All allegations — pre- or post-publication — are treated confidentially.

  1. 1
    Contact the editorial team in writing with a detailed statement and supporting evidence.
  2. 2
    Inquiry phase: initial assessment to determine whether the allegation warrants further investigation.
  3. 3
    Investigation phase (if applicable): thorough examination of evidence to develop a factual record.
  4. 4
    Resolution: a decision is made and communicated to all relevant parties.
5
Plagiarism

Plagiarism in all its forms is strictly prohibited, encompassing complete appropriation of another's work, substantial copying or paraphrasing without attribution, and misrepresentation of others' research findings.

All submitted manuscripts are screened using Crossref Similarity Check, powered by iThenticate. A similarity index of up to 20% is generally acceptable; however, editorial decisions are based on a qualitative assessment of the report rather than the score alone.

6
Informed consent

Should your article include an identifiable image (e.g., an individual's face) or personal information, providing proof of informed consent is mandatory. A completed consent to publish form serves as evidence for this purpose.

7
Citation manipulation
Self-citation

Excessive self-citation is discouraged. Cited works should be relevant and directly contribute to the article, not included simply to inflate citation counts.

Coercive citation

Including references solely at a reviewer's behest or without clear academic justification is unethical. If you feel pressured to include a specific reference, please raise your concerns.

Citation pushing

Including irrelevant references to artificially boost citation counts is monitored actively. CTUJoISD follows COPE guidelines and will investigate any suspected instances rigorously.

8
Article withdrawal, retraction, removal, replacement
Withdrawal

Authors may withdraw a manuscript during the publishing process for reasons such as significant errors, unreasonable review delays, or ethical breaches. Early withdrawal allows editing and resubmission elsewhere.

Retraction

Retractions address serious ethical breaches such as plagiarism or data fabrication. A clear retraction note is published in both print and online versions, and the original article remains accessible marked as "retracted".

Removal

Articles are almost never removed. Exceptions include legal violations, court orders, or serious risk of harm. Title and authors remain visible with a removal explanation.

Replacement

If an article could cause serious harm, authors may replace it with a corrected version. Both the new and old versions remain accessible alongside a correction timeline.
