1
Duties of authors
Reporting standards

Authors of original research must present a factual account of their work and objectively discuss its significance. The manuscript should offer enough detail for any qualified researcher to replicate the findings. Fraudulent or intentionally misleading statements are not tolerated. Reviews and editorial pieces must likewise uphold accuracy and objectivity.

Data access and retention

Authors may be requested to provide research data during editorial review. Authors should be prepared to grant public access to their data when feasible, and ensure its retention for a reasonable period following publication.

Originality and acknowledgment of sources

Authors must ensure their work is original. Any use of others' ideas, words, or data must be appropriately cited, quoted, and credited. Obtaining required permissions is paramount.

Multiple, redundant or concurrent publication

Duplicate publication — publishing essentially the same research in multiple primary journals — is unacceptable. Submitting the same manuscript to multiple journals concurrently is a particularly egregious form of this misconduct. Some specific types (e.g., clinical guidelines or translations) may be published in multiple journals under explicit agreement, with clear citation of the primary work.

Confidentiality

Information entrusted during confidential services such as reviewing manuscripts or grant applications is strictly confidential. Disclosing or utilizing this information without express written consent constitutes a grave breach of professional ethics.

Authorship of the paper

Authorship should be reserved for those who made significant contributions to the research. The corresponding author is responsible for ensuring accurate co-authorship and that all listed authors have reviewed and approved the final manuscript. All authors collectively share responsibility for the work.

Declaration of competing interests

Authors should disclose any financial or personal ties that could be perceived as inappropriately biasing their work, including employment, consulting roles, stock ownership, honoraria, patent applications, and grants. Transparency regarding research funding is crucial.

Notification of fundamental errors

Authors discovering a significant error in their published work must promptly notify the journal editor and actively collaborate to retract or correct the paper. If an editor learns of such an error from a third party, the author must cooperate promptly.

Image integrity

Any image alteration beyond adjustments to brightness, contrast, or color balance is strictly prohibited. The addition, removal, or movement of features to mislead or distort data constitutes scientific misconduct.

2
Guidelines for manuscript preparation
Overall requirements

Manuscripts must not have been published or submitted elsewhere. Maximum 15 pages, consecutively numbered. Required sections: Title, Authors, Affiliations, Abstract, Keywords, Introduction, Material and Methods, Results and Discussion, Conclusions, Acknowledgments (if any), and References. No footnotes or appendices required.

Heading levels

Level 1 Uppercase, bold — 1. INTRODUCTION
Level 2 Lowercase, bold, first letter capitalized — 2.1. The shallots and the pathogen isolate
Level 3 Lowercase, italicized, first letter capitalized — 2.4.1. Preparation of the ligand file
1. Title

Specific, concise, and informative. 10–12 words. Avoid abbreviations and formulas. Capitalize first word, proper nouns, and words after hyphen or colon.
Example: Effect of levamisole on parameters of immune response and bacterial resistance of striped catfish (Pangasianodon hypophthalmus)

2. Authors and Affiliations

Write each author's full name without degrees. Use superscript numbers for affiliations. Mark (*) for corresponding author.
Example: To Nguyen Phuoc Mai¹, Ly Van Khanh²*, Bui Lan Anh³ — *Correspondence: lvkhanh@ctu.edu.vn

3. Abstract

No more than 200 words. Briefly state purpose, methods, results, and conclusions. No undefined abbreviations. If abbreviations are essential (≥3 times), define at first mention.

4. Keywords

4–6 keywords for indexing. Italicized, alphabetical order, separated by commas. Avoid general/plural terms.
Example: Keywords: catfish farming, clarias, hybrid catfish, inter-specific hybridization

5. Introduction

Provide background to support research motivation. Briefly review literature, summarize current knowledge, identify knowledge gaps. State clear research questions and objectives.

6. Material and Methods

Describe materials and methods in sufficient detail to allow reproduction. Summarize previously published methods with references. Describe any modifications to existing methods.

7. Results and Discussion

Present findings clearly without extrapolating results. Present results in logical order. Do not duplicate data among figures, tables, and text. Provide balanced review of relevant literature including inconsistent findings. Discuss study limitations.

8. Conclusion

State the meaning and implications of the study. Propose orientation for future research. Do not repeat research results.

9. Acknowledgment

Acknowledge funding organizations or anyone who provided intellectual, technical, or material assistance. Write names of funding organizations in full.

10. Abbreviations

Use an abbreviation only if it appears at least three times in the text. Define at its first appearance, then use the abbreviation consistently thereafter.

11. Figures and Tables

Must be clearly labeled and described by their legend. Numbered consecutively starting with Fig. 1 or Table 1. Table legends go above the table; figure legends go below the figure. Tables are divided into a maximum of 7 data columns.

12. Nomenclature and Units

Follow SI units. A space is required between number and unit (e.g., 5 L, 5 kg), except for % and temperature (e.g., 5%, 20°C). Decimal accuracy rules: ≥100 → integer; ≥10 → 1 decimal; ≥1 → 2 decimals; <1 → as needed.

13. Scientific Nomenclature

Identify all biota by their scientific names at first mention. Abbreviate genus name from second mention (e.g., Cyprinus carpio → C. carpio).

14. Math Formulas

Submit equations as editable text. Use solidus (/) for small fractions (e.g., X/Y). Use MS Word Equation or MathType for complex formulas.

15. In-text Citation (APA format)

Follow APA format using author's last name and year of publication.

Type Parenthetical Narrative
One author (Harris, 2014) Harris (2014)
Two authors (Leitch & Rushton, 2019) Leitch and Rushton (2019)
Three or more (Aron et al., 2019) Aron et al. (2019)
Group author (with abbr.) First: (Food and Agriculture Organization [FAO], 1977)
Subsequent: (FAO, 1977) 		First: Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO, 1977)
Subsequent: FAO (1977)
Multiple works (Malory, 2017; Neuburger, 2018) — chronological Malory (2017) and Neuburger (2018)
Same author, same year (Bartolini, 2000a, 2000b) Bartolini (2000a, 2000b)
Secondary source (Garrison, 2011, as cited in Kattoua et al., 2016) Garrison (2011, as cited in Kattoua et al., 2016)
16. Reference List (APA format)

Include only sources cited in the paper. Alphabetize by first author's last name. For multiple works by same author, list chronologically. All lines after the first of each entry should be indented.

Common reference formats

Journal article: Author, A. A. (Year). Title. Journal Name, volume(issue), pages. DOI

Book: Author, A. A. (Year). Title of work. Publisher.

Chapter in edited book: Author, A. A. (Year). Title. In E. Editor (Ed.), Book title (pp. xx–xx). Publisher.

Thesis/Dissertation: Author, A. A. (Year). Title (doctoral dissertation). Institution.

Website: Author, A. A. (Year, Month Day). Title. URL
